Israel Palestine Conflict

Shaurya Mahajan & Sidhi Dwivedi

Historical Analysis of the Israel – Palestine conflict


INTRODUCTION:
The word Palestine is derived from the Greek word Philistia, which dates back to the description of the region by ancient Greek writers in the 12th century BC. C. From the fall of the Ottoman Empire in World War I until 1948, Palestine generally refers to the geographic area between the Mediterranean Sea and the Jordan River. Since the early 1900s, the Arabs who call this territory home have been called Palestinians. Now, most of this land is now considered Israel. At present, Palestine theoretically includes the West Bank (the territory between present-day Israel and Jordan) and the Gaza Strip (which borders present-day Israel and Egypt). However, control of the region is a complex and evolving situation. There is no international consensus on borders, and many areas claimed by the Palestinians have been occupied by Israel for many years. More than 135 member states of the United Nations recognize Palestine as an independent country, but Israel and some other countries, including the United States, do not distinguish this. Israel\\u2013 The Palestine conflict is a very obvious conflict in the 70 years since the establishment of the State of Israel in 1948, but in fact, the roots of the conflict can be traced back at least 1,000 years ago. Palestine is the birthplace of Judaism and Christianity, and is controlled by many kingdoms and powers, including ancient Egypt, Persia, Alexander the Great and his successors, the Roman Empire, various Muslim dynasties and crusaders. In modern times, the area was ruled by the Ottoman Empire and then by the British, and since 1948 it has been divided into Israel, the West Bank and the Gaza Strip. This area is one of the first areas in the world to see human settlements, agricultural communities and civilization. After the Muslim conquest of Palestine in 636, several Muslim ruling dynasties have succeeded in their struggle to control Palestine. Umayyads, who built the Dome of the Rock and the Al-Aqsa Mosque in Jerusalem; the Abbasids; the half-torturous Ixidids; Fatimids; In 1099, the Crusaders established the Kingdom of Jerusalem in Palestine, and the Ayubid Sultanate conquered it in 1187. Despite further attempts, the Crusaders were unable to retake Palestine. The Egyptian Mamluks seized Palestine from Mongolia (which conquered the Ayubid Sultanate) in 1260. The Ottoman Empire occupied Palestine in 1516 and ruled it until Egypt occupied it in 1832. Eight years later, Britain intervened and returned the area to the Ottoman Empire. During the 19th century, with the regional migration of the Druze, Circassian and Bedouin tribes, the population changed considerably. The rise of Zionism also brought many Jewish immigrants from Europe and the revival of Hebrew. Therefore, the fact is that the Palestinian areas are culturally and religiously important to people belonging to Christianity, Islam, and Judaism. However, in contradiction with many statements, although religion also plays an important role, the issues raised today are not entirely religious or cultural issues, but territorial disputes. During the First World War, the British signed the Balfour Declaration in favor of establishing a national residence for Jews in Palestine. Soon after, Britain seized Palestine from the Ottoman Empire. The League of Nations granted Britain binding powers on Palestine in 1922. Large-scale Jewish immigration and colonial rule led to sectarian violence between Jews and Arabs, which eventually prompted the British government to announce its intention to terminate the mission in 1947. The United Nations General Assembly recommended that Palestine be divided into two countries. An Arab and a Jew. However, the situation in Palestine has deteriorated into a civil war between Arabs and Jews. The Arabs rejected the zoning plan. The Jews apparently accepted the plan and declared the independence of the State of Israel in May 1948 after the termination of the British mission. The nearby Arab countries intervened in the war, but Israel not only won, but also conquered more mission territories than envisaged in the Zoning Plan. During the war, if the Palestinians were called "Naqaba" ("catastrophe"), 700,000 people (approximately 80% of all Palestinians) fled or were conquered and deported by Israel, and were not allowed to return. Beginning in the late 1940s and over decades, approximately 850,000 Jews from the Arab world ("did Aria") emigrated to Israel. After the war, only two groups of Palestinians remained under Arab control: the West Bank (and East Jerusalem) annexed by Jordan and the Gaza Strip (occupied by Egypt), and Israel was conquered by Israel in 1967 during the Six Day War. . Despite international opposition, Israel began to establish settlements in these occupied territories. At the same time, the Palestinian National Movement gradually gained international recognition, thanks in large part to the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO, established in 1965) led by Yasser Arafat. In 1993, the "Oslo Peace Agreement" between Israel and the PLO established the Palestinian National Authority as a temporary agency to administer parts of Gaza and the West Bank (but not East Jerusalem) until the conflict was finally resolved. No new peace situation has been approved or implemented. In recent history, the relationship between Israel and the Palestinians has been characterized by repeated military conflicts, especially with Hamas, the Islamic group that also rejects the Palestinian Authority. In 2007, Hamas gained control of Gaza from the Palestinian Authority, which is now restricted to the West Bank. In November 2012, the State of Palestine (the name used by the Palestinian Authority) became a non-member observer state of the United Nations, allowing it to participate in debates in the General Assembly and increasing its chances of joining other United Nations agencies.

General background:
Now, the current conflict can be directly traced back to the establishment of Israel in 1948 after the end of the Second World War. This conflict originated from the inter-community violence between Israelis and Arabs in forced Palestine since 1920, and full-scale hostilities broke out in the civil war in 1947. The conflict continues to this day at all levels. After the war and the establishment of the United Nations, the United Nations proposed a plan to divide Palestine into two parts:-an independent Jewish state and an independent Arab state. The city of Jerusalem was once called the capital by Palestinian Jews and Arabs, and it is an international territory with a special status. The proposal was accepted by Jewish leaders, but rejected by Palestinian Arabs who had strongly opposed British and Jewish interests since the 1920s. Arab groups argued that they were part of a larger population in certain territories, so they demanded more territories. And began to form volunteers. army.

The Republic of Israel:
In May 1948, Israel declared itself an independent country, accepted the zoning plan, and the British withdrew from the area. After Israel announced this declaration, five Arab countries: Jordan, Egypt, Syria, Iraq and Lebanon declared war on Israel and surrendered their troops. When the war ended in July 1949, Israel controlled more than two-thirds of what was allowed by former Britain, while Jordan controlled the West Bank, Egypt and the Gaza Strip. The 1948 conflict opened a new chapter in the struggle between Palestinian Jews and Arabs. Now this fight has turned into a regional race involving nationstates and diplomatic, political, and economic interests. Establishment of the Palestine Liberation Organization: - The Palestine Liberation Organization was established in 1964 with the purpose of establishing a Palestinian Arab state on the land previously authorized by the United Kingdom. The PLO believes that this is illegally occupied by the State of Israel. Initially, the PLO promised to destroy the State of Israel in order to achieve its goal of establishing a Palestinian state. In the 1993 Oslo Agreement, the PLO accepted Israel\u2019s right to exist in exchange for Israel\u2019s official recognition of the PLO.

Six-Day War:
The Six-Day War took place in 1967. As the name suggests, the war ended in six years and Israel won a decisive victory. The war took place at a time of turbulent diplomatic relations and conflicts between Israel and the borders of its neighbors. The conflict occurred between Israel and Egypt, Syria and Jordan, and was also related to certain intelligence provided by the Soviet Union to Egypt, which was later found to be false. Based on this information, Egypt handed over its assets to Israel and in the process withdrew from United Nations peacekeeping forces in the region for about ten years. Israel carried out a pre-emptive air strike against Egypt, and the two countries claimed that the right to self-defense contained in Article 51 of the United Nations Charter was the reason for their actions. Before the end of the war, Israel controlled the Gaza Strip, the West Bank, the Sinai Peninsula (the desert area between the Mediterranean and the Red Sea), and the Golan Heights (the rocky plateau between Syria and modern Israel). The outcome of the 1967 Arab-Israeli war will lead to decades of continuing tension and armed conflict between Israel and its neighbors.

The first agreement between INFITADA and Oslo:
In 1987, the Palestinians erupted in anger due to the Israeli occupation of Gaza and the West Bank. The Palestinian militia launched a rebellion and hundreds of people were killed. The subsequent peace process, known as the “Oslo Peace Agreement”, began in the early 1990s with the aim of ending multilateral efforts to end the current violence. The first “Oslo Agreement” (“Oslo One”) set the timetable for the Middle East peace process and laid out plans for the interim Palestinian government in Gaza and parts of the West Bank. The agreement was signed in 1993 and was witnessed by Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin and Palestinian leader Yasser Arafat. After 27 years in exile, Arafat returned to Gaza in 1994. He led the newly formed Palestinian Authority. In 1995, Oslo II laid the foundation for the complete withdrawal of Israeli troops from parts of the West Bank and other areas. He also developed a calendar for the election of the Palestinian Legislative Council. Unfortunately, even if Israel and Palestine have agreed on a comprehensive peace plan, the Oslo Agreement has not achieved its ultimate goal.

Second INFITADA:
In September 2000, the second Palestinian uprising began. One of the reasons for the violence was that Ariel Sharon, a rightwing Israeli Jew who later became the prime minister of Israel, visited the Muslim shrine in the Al-Aqsa Mosque in Jerusalem. Many Palestinians thought this was an offensive measure and protested. Then riots broke out, suicide bombings and other attacks ended the hopeful peace process. This period of violence between Palestinians and Israelis lasted almost five years. Yasser Arafat died in November 2004 and, in August 2005, the Israeli army withdrew from Gaza.

Hamas:
In 2006, the Sunni Islamic militant group Hamas won the Palestinian legislative elections. That same year, there was a battle between Hamas and Fatah, the political organization that controls the Palestine Liberation Organization. In 2007, Hamas defeated Fatah in the battle with Gaza. Many countries regard Hamas as a terrorist organization. The organization carried out suicide bombings and repeatedly called for the destruction of Israel. Hamas and Israel fought each other in several bloody wars, including Operation Cast Lead in December 2008, Operation Pillar of Defense in November 2012, and Operation Protect the Edge in July 2014.
